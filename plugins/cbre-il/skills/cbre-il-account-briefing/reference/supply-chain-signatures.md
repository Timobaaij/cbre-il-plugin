# Supply-chain signatures (the company-specific supply-chain read)

This reference powers the `supply-chain-signature` answer (`reference/deck-structure.md`) and the signature read in `reference/synthesis-and-analysis.md`. Its job is to make the brief say what is **structurally distinctive about THIS company's supply chain** and **what that means for how the network is designed and run** - so a CBRE I&L lead can sit across from a supply-chain, logistics or operations director and talk credibly about *their own operation*. It is **supply-chain intelligence, not a real-estate pitch.**

> **Stay on the supply chain. The real-estate translation is a DIFFERENT answer (`challenge-to-real-estate`).** Do NOT put site-selection, leasing, build-to-suit, clear-height/racking spec, or "how CBRE should approach it" on this slide. This slide names the characteristics and their consequence for the **network and the operating set-up**. The property consequence and the CBRE angle are built on top of it later, on their own slides. If you catch yourself writing "leasable space" or "site selection" here, it belongs on the next act.

It works for **ANY operation** - retailer, e-commerce, B2B distributor, OR manufacturer. The lens flexes: for a retailer the dominant traits are usually assortment, seasonality and channel; for a manufacturer they are make-vs-buy depth, component inbound dependency, and production rhythm. It is a lens, not a template: assess the dimensions, name the dominant two to four, identify the archetype, state the network/operating consequence, and contrast to a different archetype so the read is never generic. Every characteristic is sourced or flagged as an inference; where evidence is thin, say so.

## The slide answers three questions, in order (all supply-chain)
1. **What is structurally distinctive** about this company's supply chain - the two to four characteristics that dominate, **named in the language a supply-chain director uses** (e.g. high seasonality, long tail / high SKU variability, import-led, cold chain, vertically integrated, make-to-order, high service intensity, spare-parts long tail).
2. **What each means for the NETWORK and the SET-UP** - the supply-chain consequence of each, led with the consequence not the fact: network shape (how many nodes and their role), capacity strategy (peak-vs-base, overflow, buffering), inventory positioning (central vs forward, postponement, safety stock), automation vs labour, make-vs-buy, in-house vs 3PL, replenishment cadence. **This is the substance of the slide.** Example: "a roughly [X] peak-to-trough swing means the network is sized for weeks it barely uses, so the live question is overflow and 3PL surge, not a single steady-state operation."
3. **The archetype, the contrast, and what to probe** - name the archetype the traits add up to, contrast it to a *different* archetype in one line, and list the **supply-chain questions to probe** with the CSCO (peak ratio, SKU count, in-house-vs-3PL split, lead times, localisation roadmap). These probe questions are what make you sound like you understand their operation, and they feed `what-to-probe`.

The analytical engine is **contrast to archetype**: always state the consequence of being this type, ideally with a one-line contrast to a different type.

## The dimensions (assess each; surface only the dominant two to four) - makers and sellers alike
Each "drives" below is a **network / operating** consequence. (The real-estate consequence is deliberately omitted - it is the `challenge-to-real-estate` read.)
- **Demand profile / seasonality.** Flat vs peaky; peak-to-trough ratio; share of volume in the top weeks; promo/event spikes. Drives peak-vs-base capacity, overflow and flex, 3PL surge, seasonal labour, forecast risk.
- **Assortment: SKU count, range churn, long tail.** Breadth/depth; how fast the range turns; tail length. Drives pick complexity, slotting, slow-mover handling (central vs forward), the automation business case, safety stock. *(For a maker: variant/BOM complexity and where variety is added.)*
- **Value density and cube.** Value per pallet / cubic metre; bulky-light vs small-dense; weight/fragility. Drives cost-led vs speed-led network, consolidation economics, transport mode, security.
- **Temperature and shelf-life.** Ambient / chilled / frozen / multi-temperature; perishability and date-code pressure. Drives cold-chain tiers, replenishment cadence, proximity to demand, waste.
- **Make-vs-buy and vertical-integration depth.** *(Often dominant for manufacturers.)* How many stages of the chain the company controls vs outsources. Drives what a regional/localised network must *replicate*, supplier dependency, and where localisation is a make-vs-buy decision per link.
- **Inbound / sourcing and import profile.** Import-led and port-centric vs domestically produced/sourced; lead times; container/vessel cadence; supplier concentration. Drives port proximity vs domestic inbound, inbound consolidation and buffering, exposure to lead-time shocks.
- **Production / replenishment rhythm and postponement.** *(Often dominant for manufacturers.)* Make-to-stock vs make-to-order; production cadence; where variety is added. Drives finished-goods buffer, sequencing, plant-attached vs regional storage, postponement strategy.
- **Service proposition.** B2B pallet vs B2C eaches; next-day / same-day; store-replenishment cadence; click-and-collect; SLA. Drives the number and location of nodes and the last-mile model.
- **Channel mix.** Wholesale / retail / e-commerce / DTC; single-channel vs omnichannel. Drives the fulfilment model and whether a separate fast node is needed.
- **Returns intensity.** High (apparel, electronics) vs near-zero (grocery, cards, capital goods). Drives returns-processing capacity, reverse-logistics design, grading.

## The archetypes (illustrative, not exhaustive; companies often blend two; covers makers and sellers)
| Archetype | Dominant drivers | Network shape | Operating / set-up consequence | What to probe (supply chain) |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Seasonal / event-driven** (cards, toys, gifting, garden, confectionery) | Extreme peaks on a low base; long SKU tail; low value density | Base estate + overflow; heavy 3PL surge | Sized for weeks it barely uses; leans on flex/overflow + 3PL surge over one right-sized op; seasonality undercuts the automation case, pushing labour-dense manual | Peak-to-trough ratio; share of volume in top weeks; current overflow/3PL arrangement; in-house-vs-3PL split at peak |
| **Fast-fashion / newness-driven** (apparel, footwear) | High SKU churn and drops; high returns; dual-speed | Dual-speed network; returns hubs; urban DTC nodes | Speed-to-store + e-com flow; big returns-processing operation; newness drives constant re-slotting | Drop cadence; returns rate and processing model; e-com share; store-replenishment frequency |
| **Omnichannel grocery / perishable** | Cold chain; short shelf-life; daily replenishment; dense coverage | Dense, multi-temperature network; MFC / dark stores near population | Multi-temperature handling; very high replenishment frequency; waste/date-code discipline | Temperature mix; replenishment cadence; MFC/dark-store strategy; waste rate |
| **High-value small-cube** (electronics, pharma, jewellery) | High value density; security; often air freight; regulated (GDP for pharma) | Fewer, secure central nodes; forward stocking for service | Security and compliance run the operation; central-vs-forward inventory balance; mode choice (air vs ocean) | Security/compliance regime; central-vs-forward split; air-vs-ocean mix; serialisation |
| **Bulky low-value** (furniture, DIY, large appliances) | High cube; two-man / white-glove delivery; lower stock turns | Hub-and-spoke; cross-dock; regional big-box | Cube drives transport and handling; two-man last mile; cross-dock to cut handling | Cube profile; delivery model (two-man/white-glove); cross-dock use; turns |
| **Spare parts / service logistics** (industrial, automotive aftermarket) | Very long-tail SKUs; SLA-driven availability | Central master + many small forward stocking points | Availability/fill-rate is the whole game; long tail held centrally, fast-movers pushed forward | SLA/fill-rate targets; forward-stocking footprint; tail-vs-fast-mover split |
| **Vertically integrated maker** (esp. mid-reshoring) | Controls many chain stages in-house; import-led inbound; localisation under tariff/policy pressure | Must replicate multiple stages regionally; port-centric inbound; growing local distribution layer | Localisation is a make-vs-buy decision per link; dual-run with imports through ramp; co-locate suppliers for local content; outsourced distribution scales with sales | Localisation roadmap per link; plant ramp schedule + local-content target; inbound/vessel cadence; in-house-vs-3PL distribution and parts |
| **Make-to-stock / make-to-order manufacturer** | Raw-material/component inbound; plant-attached storage; production rhythm | Plant-attached storage + regional DCs | Inbound consolidation feeds the line; postponement and finished-goods buffer set by make-to-stock vs make-to-order | Production rhythm; component inbound dependency; finished-goods buffer; postponement point; regional DC role |
| **B2B distribution / wholesale** | Branch/depot service; trade counter; revenue-per-depot economics | Branch / depot network + central DC | Service radius and depot economics drive the network; central DC replenishes depots | Depot economics; branch rationalisation; service radius; central-vs-depot stock split |

## Writing the read (keep it analytical and on the supply chain)
- Name only the **dominant** characteristics; do not walk all the dimensions. Two to four is right.
- Lead each with the **network / operating consequence**, not the fact - and **not** the real-estate consequence.
- Close on the **archetype + a one-line contrast + the supply-chain probes** (these feed `what-to-probe`).
- Every characteristic is **sourced** (a Claim ID) or flagged as an inference; an honest gap phrased as a probe beats a guess.
- **Real estate stays off this slide.** "Site selection", "leasable space", "build-to-suit", "clear height", "the broker angle" all belong on `challenge-to-real-estate`. Here write "the network needs X / the set-up implies Y / they run it as Z".

## Worked illustration A - a seasonal, high-SKU, low-value cards/gifting company (a seller)
- **Distinctive:** extreme event-driven seasonality (Christmas plus Valentine's, Mother's Day, Easter create multi-times peaks on a low base); a very long SKU tail with constant range churn; low value density; heavy wholesale/retail replenishment plus a small, growing personalised DTC channel.
- **What it means for the network and set-up:** the whole problem is paying for peak used a few weeks a year, so the network leans on overflow/flex and 3PL surge over one right-sized operation; high-SKU pick intensity would favour goods-to-person automation, but the seasonality undercuts the automation business case, pushing toward labour-dense manual operations and seasonal labour; low value density makes the network cost-led; personalisation pulls a separate small fast node with its own flow.
- **Archetype + contrast + probe:** seasonal / event-driven. Unlike a grocery network sized to daily throughput, or a fashion player whose pain is returns and newness speed, this company's entire problem is peak it only uses for weeks. Probe the peak-to-trough ratio, the share of volume in the top eight weeks, the current overflow arrangement, and the in-house-vs-3PL split at peak.

## Worked illustration B - a vertically integrated maker, mid-reshoring (a manufacturer, e.g. a BYD-type EV maker)
- **Distinctive:** deep vertical integration (batteries, chips, motors, even ocean shipping in-house); import-led, ocean-borne inbound (finished vehicles arriving on the company's own ro-ro vessels, a lumpy vessel cadence rather than a steady flow); tariff-driven reshoring (an import duty is the delta forcing European production at speed); inland distribution and parts outsourced.
- **What it means for the network and set-up:** because the company owns most of the chain, **localisation is a make-vs-buy decision per link** - a European network has to replicate *multiple* stages (assembly, battery), not just final assembly; inbound is **port-centric and vessel-paced**, so the set-up needs port-adjacent vehicle handling and buffering sized to shipment cadence; standing up European assembly fast means **dual-running with imports through the ramp** and **co-locating suppliers** to meet local-content rules; the in-house-ships / outsourced-distribution split tells you the repeatable, scaling layer is finished-vehicle distribution and parts, run by 3PLs and growing with European sales.
- **Archetype + contrast + probe:** vertically integrated maker, mid-reshoring. Unlike a fashion retailer (pain = returns and newness) or a seasonal gifting business (pain = peak it uses for weeks), the problem here is **replicating a multi-stage chain inside a tariff wall, fast, while ramping**. Probe the localisation roadmap per link, the plant ramp schedule and local-content target, the vessel/port cadence, the supplier-park strategy, and the 3PL-vs-in-house distribution and parts model.
- *(The real-estate consequence - assembly and battery plants, port-adjacent vehicle compounds, distribution sheds, a supplier park near the plant - is the `challenge-to-real-estate` slide, NOT this one.)*
