# Supply-chain signatures (the company-specific I&L read)

This reference powers the `supply-chain-signature` answer (`reference/deck-structure.md`) and the signature read in `reference/synthesis-and-analysis.md`. Its job is to make the brief say what is **distinctive about THIS company's supply chain** and therefore how a CBRE I&L team should approach the conversation, because a highly seasonal, high-SKU greeting-card business needs a different logistics and real-estate set-up than a fast-fashion retailer, which is different again from an omnichannel grocer.

It is a lens, not a template. Assess the dimensions, name the dominant two to four, identify the archetype, and state the consequence. Only the characteristics that genuinely dominate this company reach the slide; every one is sourced or evidenced, and where the evidence is thin, say so.

## The slide answers three questions, in order
1. **What is distinctive** about this company's supply chain (the dominant characteristics, evidenced).
2. **How that shapes the set-up** (the network shape and building-spec consequence of each).
3. **How CBRE should approach it** (which notes to hit, what to probe, what kind of solution the archetype calls for).

The analytical engine is **contrast to archetype**: state the consequence of being this type, ideally with a one-line contrast to a different type, so the read is never generic.

## The dimensions (assess each; surface only the dominant ones)
- **Demand profile / seasonality.** Flat vs peaky; the peak-to-trough ratio; how concentrated the year is (share of volume in the top weeks); promotional or event spikes. Drives the peak-vs-base capacity tension, overflow and flex space, 3PL surge, and seasonal labour.
- **SKU count and range churn.** Breadth and depth of the assortment; long tail; how fast the range turns over (fashion drops vs staples). Drives pick complexity, slotting, the automation business case, and the space the range needs.
- **Value density and cube.** Value per pallet/cubic metre; bulky-light vs small-dense; weight and fragility. Drives whether locations are cost-led or speed-led, security, and clear-height/racking spec.
- **Temperature and shelf-life.** Ambient / chilled / frozen / multi-temperature; perishability and date-code pressure. Drives cold-store spec, replenishment cadence, and proximity to demand.
- **Service proposition.** B2B pallet vs B2C eaches; next-day / same-day; store-replenishment cadence; click-and-collect. Drives the number and location of nodes and the last-mile model.
- **Channel mix.** Wholesale / retail / e-commerce / DTC; single-channel vs omnichannel. Drives the fulfilment model and whether a separate fast node is needed.
- **Returns intensity.** High (apparel, electronics) vs near-zero (grocery, cards). Drives returns-processing space and reverse-logistics design.
- **Inbound / import profile.** Import-led and port-centric vs domestically produced; lead times and container flows. Drives port proximity and inbound consolidation.

## The archetypes (illustrative, not exhaustive; companies often blend two)
| Archetype | Dominant drivers | Network shape | Building / location | CBRE conversation angle |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Seasonal / event-driven** (greeting cards, toys, gifting, garden, confectionery) | Extreme peaks on a low base; long SKU tail; low value density | Peak-vs-base tension; base estate plus overflow; heavy 3PL surge | Cost-led locations near labour pools; flex/overflow space; automation ROI undercut by seasonality | Peak-flex strategy (short-term overflow, 3PL structuring), labour-availability siting, the automation-vs-flex capital question |
| **Fast-fashion / newness-driven** (apparel, footwear) | High SKU churn and drops; high returns; dual-speed (store replenishment + e-com) | Dual-speed network; returns hubs; urban DTC nodes | Speed-to-store locations; returns-processing space; some urban last-mile | Returns and reverse-logistics design, speed-to-store siting, e-com node strategy |
| **Omnichannel grocery / perishable** | Cold chain; short shelf-life; daily replenishment; dense population coverage | Dense, multi-temperature network; MFC / dark stores near population | Chilled/frozen spec; urban proximity; high replenishment frequency | Cold-store and MFC strategy, urban proximity, network densification |
| **High-value small-cube** (electronics, pharma, jewellery) | High value density; security; often air freight; regulated (GDP for pharma) | Fewer, secure central nodes; forward stocking for service | Secure, compliant, smaller footprint; airport proximity | Security and compliance spec, central-vs-forward balance, valuation |
| **Bulky low-value** (furniture, DIY, large appliances) | High cube; two-man / white-glove delivery; lower stock turns | Hub-and-spoke; cross-dock; regional big-box | High clear height, big-box, cross-dock; two-man delivery hubs | Big-box siting, cross-dock design, last-mile two-man network |
| **Spare parts / service logistics** (industrial, automotive aftermarket) | Very long-tail SKUs; SLA-driven availability | Central master plus many small forward stocking locations | Small forward sites near demand; high-density central | Forward-stocking network design, SLA-to-footprint translation |
| **Make-to-stock manufacturer** | Raw-material inbound; plant-attached warehousing; production rhythm | Plant-attached storage plus regional DCs | Inbound consolidation; plant-adjacent; regional distribution | Plant-adjacent and inbound strategy, regional DC siting, reshoring |
| **B2B distribution / wholesale** | Branch/depot service; trade counter; revenue-per-depot economics | Branch / depot network; central DC | Trade-counter plus warehouse; regional depots | Depot-network optimisation, branch rationalisation, revenue-per-depot read |

## Writing the read (keep it analytical, never a matrix)
- Name only the **dominant** characteristics; do not walk all eight dimensions. Two to four is right.
- Lead each with the **consequence**, not the fact: "a roughly [X] peak-to-trough swing means the network is sized for weeks it barely uses, so the live question is overflow and 3PL surge, not a single big-box lease."
- Close on **how CBRE should approach it** and **what to probe** (feed `what-to-probe`): the peak ratio, the share of volume in the top weeks, the current overflow arrangement and its cost, the in-house-vs-3PL split, the returns rate, the cold-chain footprint, whichever the archetype makes load-bearing.
- Every characteristic is **sourced** (a Claim ID) or flagged as an inference; an honest gap phrased as a probe beats a guess.

## Worked illustration (a seasonal, high-SKU, low-value card company)
- **Distinctive:** extreme event-driven seasonality (Christmas plus Valentine's, Mother's Day, Easter create multi-times peaks on a low base); a very long SKU tail with constant range churn; low value density; heavy wholesale/retail replenishment plus a small, growing personalised DTC channel.
- **How it shapes the set-up:** the whole problem is paying for peak used a few weeks a year, so the network leans on overflow/flex space and 3PL surge over one right-sized box; high-SKU pick intensity would favour goods-to-person automation, but the seasonality undercuts the automation business case, pushing toward labour-dense manual operations near labour pools; low value density makes locations cost-led; personalisation pulls a separate small fast node.
- **How CBRE should approach it:** lead with peak-flex strategy (short-term overflow leases, structuring the 3PL surge, the automation-vs-flex capital question) and labour-availability siting; probe the peak ratio, the share of volume in the top eight weeks, the current overflow arrangement and its cost, and the in-house-vs-3PL split at peak.
- **Contrast (the payoff line):** unlike a grocery DC sized to daily throughput or a fashion player whose pain is returns and newness speed, this company's entire real-estate problem is peak it only uses for weeks.
